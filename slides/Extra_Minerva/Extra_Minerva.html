Mnemic Psychology

Historical Sidenote

  • Modern instance theory is broadly consistent with Richard Semon’s theory of memory developed in Mnemic Psychology (1923)
  • Semon was a German theorist whose ideas were largely forgotten until they were translated to English (mid-century)
  • See Schacter, D. L., Eich, J. E., & Tulving, E. (1978). Richard Semon’s theory of memory. Journal of Verbal Learning and Verbal Behavior, 17(6), 721-743.
  • Semon’s ideas inspired Hintzman’s MINERVA 2

Instance theory primers

  • A collection of related process theories of memory and cognition
  • Different researchers have different versions (broadly similar)
  • Instance theories range in specificity from verbal theories to computational models

Big ideas from Instance theory

  1. Offers testable processing explanations for how memory works:
  • not a simple stage model
  • does not invoke special processing
  • representations are defined
  • encoding and retrieval operations are defined
  1. Shows how memory processes can do more than just remember, and may be responsible for many other cognitive abilities.

Minerva 2

  • MINERVA 2 is a global-matching model of memory (Hintzman, 1984,86,88)

General Encoding Assumption

People encode the details of their experiences in memory traces

  • Instance theory assumes very high/limitless capacity for traces
  • Every experience is potentially encoded as a trace
  • lifetime of experiences potentially available from memory, although maybe not accessible.

Individual experiences are full of features

  • What properties are in a slice of experience?

    • Sensory details of the environment (visual, audio, haptic, smell, etc.)
    • Sense of space, time
    • Co-occurring thoughts and feelings
    • Co-occurring cognitive processes
    • Co-occurring physiological processes

  • A single memory trace could theoretically contain all of the features inherent to a single experience

General Retrieval Assumption

  • Similarity between present and past drives memory retrieval

  • The present moment is a pattern of current experience

  • Memory contains many traces of experiences

    • each trace is the pattern of a past experience

  • Similar experiences have similar patterns

  • The pattern of current experience causes retrieval of past experiences that have similar patterns.

Recap

  1. People encode the details of all their individual experiences
  2. Single experience are full of co-occuring features
  3. Retrieval is similarity driven.

  • the pattern in the present moment retrieves experiences with similar patterns from the past

  • These assumptions could have interesting and testable implications for cognition.

Theoretical strategy

The instance theory approach is to:

  1. Clarify the meaning of the assumptions
  2. Postulate algorithms about how representations (traces) and processes (retrieval by similarity) may work together
  3. Determine whether a cognitive ability or phenomenon of interest could emerge from the working assumptions of instance theory
  4. When instance theory works, it offers a candidate explanation of the cognitive phenomena in question.

MINERVA

  • MINERVA is a computational model
  • It implements some assumptions of instance theory
  • The model stores and retrieves patterns
  • The model can be trained on patterns that people respond to in cognitive psychology experiments
  • The model can produce measurable responses
  • Model performance can be compared to human/animal performance
